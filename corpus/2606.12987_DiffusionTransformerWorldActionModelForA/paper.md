###### Abstract

We study compact, action-conditioned world models for autonomous
driving. Given the current front-camera latent and a sequence of
ego-actions, our system predicts future scene latents that a frozen
decoder renders to $`256{\times}256`$ frames up to 8 seconds ahead. We
evaluate all models on 150 held-out nuScenes driving scenes across
diverse urban conditions in Boston and Singapore. We first benchmark
*where* to predict: across six frozen visual encoders spanning four
representation families, V-JEPA2 with temporal context reduces steering
RMSE by 40% relative to the best single-frame encoder. We then build a
latent Diffusion Transformer (DiT) and, through a controlled
hypothesis-driven diagnosis, identify the four ingredients it needs:
spatial tokens, the $`x_{0}`$ prediction objective, residual anchoring,
and sampling matched to target uncertainty. Moving to a
Stable-Diffusion-VAE encode-predict-decode pipeline, we surface a
central tension. Standard distortion metrics (cosine similarity, SSIM)
*favor the blurry regression mean*, masking the fact that the diffusion
model is far closer to the real frame distribution. Measuring with
Inception-based FID and KID reveals a clean
<span class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">perception-distortion
frontier</span>: the diffusion model attains KID $`0.078`$
versus $`0.375`$ for direct regression ($`4.8\times`$ better), and a
<span class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">deployable train-derived
calibration</span> makes this practical without access to test-time
ground truth. The model is genuinely *action*-controllable: steering
monotonically drives predicted scene displacement (Spearman
$`\rho{=}0.81`$) where the regression baseline is uncorrelated
($`\rho{=}{-}0.18`$). We diagnose limited single-pass temporal motion to
a shared-present anchor and show that a compact 1.7 M-parameter “jump”
world model recovers full ground-truth motion magnitude ($`1.02\times`$
GT) on held-out scenes, where single-pass models capture less than half.
The result is a small, controllable AV world model with a realism
frontier and a validated route to coarse temporal motion; higher
appearance fidelity scales with capacity and data.

</div>

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">1 </span>Introduction

<figure id="S1.F1" class="ltx_figure">
<img src="2606.12987v1/x1.png" id="S1.F1.g1"

<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_figure"><span id="S1.F1.6.3.1"
class="ltx_text" style="font-size:90%;">Figure 1</span>: </span><span
id="S1.F1.4.2" class="ltx_text" style="font-size:90%;">Single-pass
architecture. A frozen SD-VAE encodes the present front-camera frame to
a <span class="math inline">32 × 32 × 4</span> latent grid; ego-actions
are embedded via learned Fourier features. The Anchored VAE DiT (4
blocks, adaLN-Zero conditioning) predicts future latent tokens as
residuals from the present anchor. The frozen VAE decoder renders the

frames. In diffusion mode, the input is noised future latents; in direct
(regression) mode, the present latent is replicated.</span></figcaption>
</figure>

<div id="S1.p1" class="ltx_para">

World models for autonomous driving predict future scene representations
conditioned on ego-actions, enabling planning and simulation without
acting in the environment \[<a href="#bib.bib12" class="ltx_ref">12</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib14" class="ltx_ref">14</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib23" class="ltx_ref">23</a>\]. Yet two questions precede
any such model: which latent space to predict in, and whether a
generative transformer adds value over a deterministic regressor in that
space.

</div>

<div id="S1.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S1.p2.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Input and
output.</span> The input to our system is the current front-camera
frame, encoded to a Stable-Diffusion-VAE
latent \[<a href="#bib.bib19" class="ltx_ref">19</a>\], together with a
sequence of logged ego-actions (steering angle, acceleration). A latent
Diffusion Transformer
(DiT) \[<a href="#bib.bib16" class="ltx_ref">16</a>\] world-action model
outputs predicted future scene latents at up to 16 horizon steps (8 s at
2 Hz), which the frozen VAE decoder renders back to $`256{\times}256`$
frames (Figure <a href="#S1.F1" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">1</span></a>).

</div>

<div id="S1.p3" class="ltx_para">

We answer the first question with a systematic benchmark of six frozen
encoders spanning supervised, self-supervised image, self-supervised
video, and reconstruction families.
V-JEPA2 \[<a href="#bib.bib3" class="ltx_ref">3</a>\] with temporal
context dramatically outperforms all single-frame alternatives. We
answer the second through a controlled, hypothesis-driven diagnosis that
isolates *when* a DiT helps: the critical factors are the prediction
objective ($`x_{0}`$ vs. $`\epsilon`$), spatial structure, residual
anchoring, and the match between sampling and target uncertainty.

</div>

<div id="S1.p4" class="ltx_para">

The core of the paper is what happens in the production-style VAE
encode-predict-decode pipeline, where appearance ambiguity under point
losses becomes pronounced. A deterministic regressor wins every
*distortion* metric by collapsing to a blurry conditional mean. Yet this
is exactly the <span id="S1.p4.1.2"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">perception-distortion
tradeoff</span> \[<a href="#bib.bib5" class="ltx_ref">5</a>\]:
distribution metrics
(FID/KID \[<a href="#bib.bib9" class="ltx_ref">9</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref">4</a>\]) reveal the diffusion model
is far closer to the real frame distribution. We characterize this
frontier, make the diffusion advantage *deployable* via a train-derived
calibration, demonstrate action controllability and show a
reparameterization that recovers forward-motion direction.\
\

</div>

<div id="S1.p5" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S1.p5.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Contributions.</span>

1.  <span id="S1.I1.i1"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">1.</span></span>

    </div>
2.  <span id="S1.I1.i2"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">2.</span></span>

    </div>
3.  <span id="S1.I1.i3"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">3.</span></span>

<div id="S2" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">2 </span>Related Work

<div id="S2.p1" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S2.p1.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">AV world
models.</span> GAIA-1 \[<a href="#bib.bib12" class="ltx_ref">12</a>\]
generates future driving video from text and actions via an
autoregressive transformer.
Cosmos \[<a href="#bib.bib14" class="ltx_ref">14</a>\] and
MovieGen-style video
priors \[<a href="#bib.bib17" class="ltx_ref">17</a>\] scale latent
video generation to internet-scale data.
DriveDreamer \[<a href="#bib.bib25" class="ltx_ref">25</a>\] and
GenAD \[<a href="#bib.bib26" class="ltx_ref">26</a>\] couple traffic
structure with diffusion for driving-video synthesis.
DIAMOND \[<a href="#bib.bib1" class="ltx_ref">1</a>\] applies diffusion
world dynamics to learn environment simulators for Atari game control.
UniSim \[<a href="#bib.bib24" class="ltx_ref">24</a>\] builds
interactive real-world simulators from diverse data sources. These
systems typically operate in pixel or high-dimensional spatial-token
latent spaces at large scale. Concurrent work by
Shi et al. \[<a href="#bib.bib20" class="ltx_ref">20</a>\] demonstrates
DiT world-action models at 5 B parameters with video priors; our study
instead operates in the *compact* regime and asks which design factors
and evaluation metrics matter, contributing controlled analysis rather
than a scaled system.

</div>

<div id="S2.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S2.p2.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Visual encoders for
driving.</span> DINOv2 \[<a href="#bib.bib15" class="ltx_ref">15</a>\]
provides self-distilled image features;
CLIP \[<a href="#bib.bib18" class="ltx_ref">18</a>\] learns
vision-language aligned representations; V-JEPA and
V-JEPA2 \[<a href="#bib.bib2" class="ltx_ref">2</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib3" class="ltx_ref">3</a>\] learn predictive video
representations by masking and reconstructing latent features across
time; VQGAN \[<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref">8</a>\] produces
reconstruction-oriented codebook tokens. Prior driving work typically
fixes one encoder without systematic comparison; we evaluate across
families and quantify the advantage of temporal video representations.

</div>

<div id="S2.p3" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S2.p3.2.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Diffusion
transformers and the perception-distortion tradeoff.</span>
DiT \[<a href="#bib.bib16" class="ltx_ref">16</a>\] applies transformer
blocks with adaptive layer normalization (adaLN-Zero) for
class-conditioned image generation, demonstrating scalability with
compute. DDIM \[<a href="#bib.bib21" class="ltx_ref">21</a>\] enables
deterministic sampling; the $`\epsilon`$ vs. $`x_{0}`$ objective affects
training stability \[<a href="#bib.bib10" class="ltx_ref">10</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib13" class="ltx_ref">13</a>\]; classifier-free
guidance \[<a href="#bib.bib11" class="ltx_ref">11</a>\] trades
diversity for conditioning fidelity; Fourier
features \[<a href="#bib.bib22" class="ltx_ref">22</a>\] embed
continuous conditioning signals. Crucially, the <span id="S2.p3.2.2"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">perception-distortion
tradeoff</span> \[<a href="#bib.bib5" class="ltx_ref">5</a>\] proves
that minimizing distortion (MSE/cosine similarity) and maximizing
perceptual realism are fundamentally at odds.
FID \[<a href="#bib.bib9" class="ltx_ref">9</a>\] and
KID \[<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref">4</a>\] measure the latter via
Inception feature statistics. This lens is standard in generative vision
but rare in AV latent prediction, where cosine similarity and L2 remain
the default metrics. We bridge this gap by applying distribution-based
evaluation to driving world models.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S3" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">3 </span>Dataset and Features

<div id="S3.p1" class="ltx_para">

We use the nuScenes v1.0-trainval
dataset \[<a href="#bib.bib6" class="ltx_ref">6</a>\], comprising 850
driving scenes (approximately 20 s each) captured at 2 Hz keyframe rate
across diverse urban environments in Boston and Singapore, totaling
33,552 keyframes with synchronized camera and CAN-bus data.

</div>

<div id="S3.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S3.p2.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Splits.</span> We
partition *by scene* into 630 training / 70 validation / 150 test,
ensuring no scene appears in multiple splits. All metrics are reported
on the 150 held-out test scenes.

</div>

<div id="S3.p3" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S3.p3.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Actions.</span>
Ego-vehicle actions are extracted from CAN-bus data as 2D vectors
$`a_{t}=(\text{steer}_{t},\text{accel}_{t})`$, z-score normalized using
training-set statistics only.

</div>

<div id="S3.p4" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S3.p4.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Image features and
latent encoding.</span> For the encoder benchmark
(Section <a href="#S5.SS1" class="ltx_ref"
title="5.1 Encoder Benchmark ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">5.1</span></a>), each keyframe is encoded
to a 384-d pooled vector; encoders with different output dimensions use
a frozen random orthogonal projection to 384-d, preserving distances
without learnable parameters. V-JEPA2 (rep64 variant) ingests 16-frame
clips via the full video
transformer \[<a href="#bib.bib3" class="ltx_ref">3</a>\], while the
rep1 variant uses only the spatial encoder on a single frame.

</div>

<div id="S3.p5" class="ltx_para">

For the world model (Sections <a href="#S5.SS3" class="ltx_ref"
title="5.3 Perception-Distortion Frontier ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">5.3</span></a>–<a href="#S5.SS4" class="ltx_ref"
title="5.4 Motion: Diagnosis and a Compact Fix ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">5.4</span></a>), we encode CAM_FRONT frames
at $`256{\times}256`$ resolution with the frozen Stable-Diffusion
VAE \[<a href="#bib.bib19" class="ltx_ref">19</a>\] to
$`32{\times}32{\times}4`$ latent grids (scaling factor 0.18215). Latents
are patchified with patch size 4 to produce $`8{\times}8{=}64`$ spatial
tokens of dimension 64 each. Actions are embedded with learned Fourier
features \[<a href="#bib.bib22" class="ltx_ref">22</a>\] (64 frequencies
per dimension). Figure <a href="#A1.F10" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 10 ‣ Appendix A Additional Qualitative Results ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">10</span></a> (Appendix) shows
representative nuScenes frames and their VAE reconstructions.

</div>

<div id="S3.p6" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S3.p6.4.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Multi-horizon
targets.</span> Prediction targets span horizons $`H\in\{4,8,16\}`$ (2,
4, 8 s at 2 Hz). Each example comprises a current latent $`z_{t}`$,
action sequence $`\{a_{t+1},\dots,a_{t+H}\}`$, and future latents
$`\{z_{t+1},\dots,z_{t+H}\}`$. Evaluation uses scene-level aggregation:
predictions within each test scene are averaged before computing final
metrics, preventing false independence from overlapping windows.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS1" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">4.1 </span>Encoder Benchmark

<div id="S4.SS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

We evaluate six frozen encoders spanning four representation families
(Table <a href="#S3.T1" class="ltx_ref"
title="Table 1 ‣ 3 Dataset and Features ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">1</span></a>):
ViT-S/16 \[<a href="#bib.bib7" class="ltx_ref">7</a>\] (supervised
ImageNet-1k),
DINOv2-S/14 \[<a href="#bib.bib15" class="ltx_ref">15</a>\]
(self-distilled, no labels),
CLIP-B/32 \[<a href="#bib.bib18" class="ltx_ref">18</a>\]
(vision-language contrastive), V-JEPA2 in single-frame (rep1) and
16-frame-clip (rep64)
configurations \[<a href="#bib.bib3" class="ltx_ref">3</a>\], and VQ-VAE
Tracker \[<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref">8</a>\]
(reconstruction-oriented codebook). A shared 2-layer MLP probe
($`384{\to}256`$ with GELU$`\to`$<!-- -->2) predicts steering and
acceleration from each encoder’s frozen embeddings (Adam,
lr $`10^{-3}`$, batch 256, 50 epochs, 3 seeds). We report per-scene
steering and acceleration RMSE on 150 test scenes, with 95% confidence
intervals via bootstrap.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">4.2 </span>Latent DiT World-Action Model

<div id="S4.SS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS2.p1.2.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Architecture.</span> Our AnchoredVAEDiT
comprises 4 transformer blocks with 4 attention heads and model
dimension 256 ($`\sim`$<!-- -->5.4 M parameters). Each block uses
adaLN-Zero conditioning \[<a href="#bib.bib16" class="ltx_ref">16</a>\]:
given conditioning vector $`c`$,

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\gamma,\beta,\alpha=\mathrm{MLP}(c),\quad h=\alpha\odot\mathrm{Attn}\!\bigl(\gamma\cdot\mathrm{LN}(x)+\beta\bigr),
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(1)</span> |

with gate $`\alpha`$ initialized to zero so each block starts as an
identity function. The model receives $`H`$ input tokens (noisy for
diffusion, or the present embedding replicated for direct regression)
and predicts the clean future sequence
$`\hat{z}_{0}=\{z_{t+1},\dots,z_{t+H}\}`$. Conditioning $`c`$ sums three
signals: a sinusoidal timestep embedding, the pooled present latent
$`z_{t}`$ (projected from patch dim to model dim), and the per-token
Fourier action embedding.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS2.p2.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Fourier action
embedding.</span> Each action pair
$`a_{k}=(\text{steer}_{k},\text{accel}_{k})`$ is embedded via learned
Fourier features \[<a href="#bib.bib22" class="ltx_ref">22</a>\]:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\mathrm{FourierEmbed}(a)=\bigl[\sin(2\pi f_{j}a),\,\cos(2\pi f_{j}a)\bigr]_{j=1}^{N_{f}}
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(2)</span> |

with $`N_{f}{=}64`$ learned frequencies per dimension, producing
per-token embeddings $`(B,H,D)`$ that vary across the horizon, so that
adaLN modulation differs at each prediction step.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.p3" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS2.p3.3.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Residual
anchoring.</span> Rather than predicting absolute future latents, the
model predicts a residual:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\hat{z}_{t+k}=z_{t}+\Delta_{k}(z_{t},\{a_{t+i}\},\tau),
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(3)</span> |

where the present $`z_{t}`$ is broadcast across all $`H`$ horizon
positions. This anchoring stabilizes early training and ensures the
model degrades gracefully to copying the present rather than to random
noise.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.p4" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS2.p4.3.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Diffusion
objective and sampling.</span> The forward process adds Gaussian noise
following a cosine
schedule \[<a href="#bib.bib13" class="ltx_ref">13</a>\] with
$`T{=}1000`$ timesteps:
$`q(\tilde{z}_{\tau}|z_{0})=\mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{\tau}}\,z_{0},\,(1{-}\bar{\alpha}_{\tau})I\bigr)`$.
We train with the $`x_{0}`$<span id="S4.SS2.p4.3.2"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">-prediction objective</span>:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\mathcal{L}_{\text{diff}}=\mathbb{E}_{\tau,\epsilon}\,\bigl\lVert\hat{z}_{0}(\tilde{z}_{\tau},c,\tau)-z_{0}\bigr\rVert_{2}^{2},
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(4)</span> |

which is critical: our diagnosis shows that $`\epsilon`$-prediction
causes collapse in compact latent spaces, while $`x_{0}`$ recovers 88.5%
of the performance gap (Section <a href="#S5.SS2" class="ltx_ref"
title="5.2 When Does a DiT Help? (Diagnosis) ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">5.2</span></a>). We apply
<span id="S4.SS2.p4.7.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">classifier-free
guidance</span> \[<a href="#bib.bib11" class="ltx_ref">11</a>\] via
action dropout ($`p{=}0.1`$), zeroing the action embedding with
probability $`p`$ during training. At inference,
DDIM \[<a href="#bib.bib21" class="ltx_ref">21</a>\] sampling with 50
deterministic steps refines predictions from pure Gaussian noise.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.p5" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS2.p5.2.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Direct
regression baseline.</span> The same architecture at $`\tau{=}0`$ (no
noise, single forward pass) serves as a strong deterministic baseline. A
matched MLP baseline ($`384{\to}512{\to}384`$, GELU, residual output)
provides a second reference.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">4.3 </span>Distribution Metrics and Calibration

<div id="S4.SS3.p1" class="ltx_para">

Standard latent-prediction work evaluates with *distortion* metrics that
measure per-sample closeness to ground truth. We additionally report
*distribution* metrics that compare the decoded-frame distribution to
the real-frame distribution:

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS3.p2.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">FID</span> \[<a href="#bib.bib9" class="ltx_ref">9</a>\]
compares Inception-v3 feature statistics:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\mathrm{FID}=\lVert\mu_{r}{-}\mu_{g}\rVert^{2}+\mathrm{Tr}\!\bigl(\Sigma_{r}{+}\Sigma_{g}{-}2(\Sigma_{r}\Sigma_{g})^{1/2}\bigr).
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(5)</span> |

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.p3" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS3.p3.2.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">KID</span> \[<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref">4</a>\],
our primary metric (robust at small $`N`$), is the squared MMD with
polynomial kernel $`k(x,y)=(\frac{1}{d}\langle x,y\rangle+1)^{3}`$. We
report KID mean and standard deviation from <span id="S4.SS3.p3.2.2"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_typewriter">torchmetrics</span>, the latter
serving as an error bar.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.p4" class="ltx_para">

Because the VAE encoder/predictor induces a small per-channel offset in
the predicted latents, we estimate a <span id="S4.SS3.p4.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">deployable calibration</span>
(per-channel mean and scale shift) on the *training split only* and
apply it at test time. This is distinct from any post-hoc use of test
statistics and makes the FID/KID improvement *deployable* in a
production system.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S4.SS4" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">4.4 </span>Motion Fidelity and the Jump Model

<div id="S4.SS4.p1" class="ltx_para">

To probe *coherent* scene motion rather than texture churn, we decode
all 16 predicted steps and decompose each consecutive-frame difference
into:

- <span id="S4.I1.i1"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S4.I1.i1.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S4.I1.i1.p1.1.1"
  class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Low-frequency:</span> Gaussian-blurred
  ($`\sigma{=}8`$, kernel 31) consecutive L2, coherent scene structure
  change.

  </div>
- <span id="S4.I1.i2"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S4.I1.i2.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S4.I1.i2.p1.1.1"
  class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">High-frequency:</span> residual
  (original minus blurred) consecutive L2, measuring texture variation.

  </div>

We also compute an image-plane displacement via horizontal and vertical
profile cross-correlation, reporting the magnitude ratio and direction
correlation versus ground truth. All metrics are evaluated on held-out
test scenes.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS4.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS4.p2.4.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Chain-anchor
jump model.</span> The single-pass model anchors every future token on
the *same* present $`z_{t}`$ (Eq. <a href="#S4.E3" class="ltx_ref"
title="In 4.2 Latent DiT World-Action Model ‣ 4 Methods ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">3</span></a>), which biases it toward
re-rendering the current scene rather than accumulating ego-motion. We
test a reparameterization: a compact (1.7 M-parameter,
$`n_{\text{blocks}}{=}2`$, dim 192) network predicts a single
$`\Delta t{=}4`$ transition $`z_{t}\to z_{t+4}`$, conditioned on the
four intervening actions (Fourier-embedded and mean-pooled). At
inference, this model is applied as a 4-step open-loop chain,
re-anchoring on its own output at each step:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
z_{t+4j}=f_{\theta}\!\bigl(z_{t+4(j-1)},\;\bar{a}_{t+4(j-1):t+4j}\bigr),\;j=1,\dots,4
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(6)</span> |

reaching $`z_{t+16}`$ in four sequential transitions. Training uses
teacher-forced ground-truth anchors; open-loop chain with the model’s
predictions is the actual test of motion fidelity.

</div>

</div>

</div>

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">5.2 </span>When Does a DiT Help? (Diagnosis)

<div id="S5.SS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

In compact pooled latents (384-d), the DiT initially underperforms an
MLP-residual baseline. A controlled diagnostic chain
(Figure <a href="#S5.F3" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 3 ‣ 5.2 When Does a DiT Help? (Diagnosis) ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">3</span></a>) tests four hypotheses:

- <span id="S5.I1.i1"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S5.I1.i1.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S5.I1.i1.p1.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">H1
  (capacity):</span> rejected. DiT-direct (no diffusion) matches MLP, so
  the architecture is not the bottleneck.

  </div>
- <span id="S5.I1.i2"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S5.I1.i2.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S5.I1.i2.p1.3.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">H2
  (objective):</span> confirmed. Switching from $`\epsilon`$- to
  $`x_{0}`$-prediction recovers 88.5% of the gap (the $`\epsilon`$
  objective collapses to near-copy in this regime).

  </div>
- <span id="S5.I1.i3"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S5.I1.i3.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S5.I1.i3.p1.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">H3
  (horizon):</span> rejected. Longer horizons do not favor DiT; the 2 Hz
  posterior is near-unimodal when conditioned on logged actions.

  </div>
- <span id="S5.I1.i4"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S5.I1.i4.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S5.I1.i4.p1.2.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">H4
  (action-seq):</span> partially confirmed. Per-token action-sequence
  conditioning benefits DiT more than MLP (interaction $`+0.007`$ to
  $`+0.020`$ CosSim on three encoders), confirming that self-attention
  exploits per-step temporal structure.

  </div>

</div>

the DiT *architecture* beats matched-parameter MLPs on both ViT

parameters, 3 seeds. This yields four ingredients, all necessary and
jointly sufficient: spatial tokens, the $`x_{0}`$ objective, residual
anchoring, and sampling matched to target uncertainty.

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">5.3 </span>Perception-Distortion Frontier

<div id="S5.SS3.p1" class="ltx_para">

In the SD-VAE pipeline, appearance ambiguity under point losses becomes
pronounced, and the two model classes separate cleanly. The
<span id="S5.SS3.p1.9.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">direct</span>
regressor wins every *distortion* metric (CosSim $`0.471`$ vs.
$`0.260`$; higher SSIM) by predicting the blurry conditional mean. The
<span id="S5.SS3.p1.9.3" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">diffusion</span>
model wins every *distribution* metric. On KID, our primary
small-$`N`$-robust measure, diffusion with the deployable train
calibration attains $`\mathbf{0.078}`$ versus $`\mathbf{0.375}`$ for
direct regression, a $`4.8\times`$ improvement. FID tells the same story
($`162.5`$ vs. $`370.8`$). Across 3 independent seeds, KID is
$`0.076{\pm}0.005`$ (Table <a href="#S5.T2" class="ltx_ref"
title="Table 2 ‣ 5.3 Perception-Distortion Frontier ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">2</span></a>,
Figure <a href="#S5.F4" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 4 ‣ 5.3 Perception-Distortion Frontier ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">4</span></a>).

</div>

<div id="S5.SS3.p2" class="ltx_para">

This is precisely the perception-distortion
tradeoff \[<a href="#bib.bib5" class="ltx_ref">5</a>\]: *distortion
metrics, standard in latent-prediction work, systematically reward the
wrong thing for world models*. Figure <a href="#A1.F10" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 10 ‣ Appendix A Additional Qualitative Results ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">10</span></a> (Appendix) makes the tradeoff
visual: the direct row is a sharpness-collapsed blur, while the
diffusion row renders a recognizable street scene with plausible
vehicles and road markings.

</div>

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">5.4 </span>Motion: Diagnosis and a Compact Fix

<div id="S5.SS4.p1" class="ltx_para">

We provide a careful accounting of *temporal* fidelity. A single
diffusion rollout reproduces per-frame texture (high-frequency fraction
$`0.98\times`$ GT) but shows limited *coherent* scene motion:
low-frequency motion fraction is only $`0.44\times`$ GT (diffusion)
compared to $`0.56\times`$ (the blurry direct model), and image-plane
displacement is near zero for diffusion
(Figure <a href="#S5.F6" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 6 ‣ 5.3 Perception-Distortion Frontier ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">6</span></a>). A motion-targeted fine-tune
(adding a temporal-difference loss
$`\lVert\Delta z_{\text{pred}}-\Delta z_{\text{GT}}\rVert^{2}`$ for 30
epochs) did not improve these numbers, indicating a structural rather
than a loss-surface issue.

</div>

<div id="S5.SS4.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S5.SS4.p2.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Diagnosis.</span> We trace this to the
shared-present anchoring (Eq. <a href="#S4.E3" class="ltx_ref"
title="In 4.2 Latent DiT World-Action Model ‣ 4 Methods ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">3</span></a>): every future token is
computed as a delta from the same $`z_{t}`$. At 2 Hz driving, this
biases the model toward re-rendering the current layout with varied
texture rather than accumulating the ego-motion that would shift the
scene forward.

</div>

<div id="S5.SS5" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">5.5 </span>Action Controllability

<div id="S5.SS5.p1" class="ltx_para">

A world model must *use* actions, not merely render plausible scenes. We
sweep the steering input across its training distribution range (5th to
95th percentile) with fixed diffusion noise and measure the induced
horizontal scene displacement at $`t{+}15`$ on 40 held-out windows.

</div>

<div id="S5.SS5.p2" class="ltx_para">

The diffusion model is monotonically controllable: Spearman
$`\rho(\text{steer},\text{shift})=\mathbf{+0.81}`$ with 100%
sign-correct on the 18/40 scenes where shift exceeds a detection
threshold (Figure <a href="#S5.F9" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 9 ‣ 5.4 Motion: Diagnosis and a Compact Fix ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">9</span></a>). The direct regression model
is uncorrelated ($`\rho{=}{-}0.18`$, 39/40 valid).

</div>

<div id="S5.SS5.p3" class="ltx_para">

Steering left versus right produces visibly different predicted futures,
providing rigorous evidence for a “world-*action*” model. This
controllability is a planning-relevant property: a non-circular
inverse-control probe (predicting the held-out steering value that
produced a given future, not evaluated on training data) finds the
diffusion model recovers target steers at $`0.67\times`$ chance error,
while the direct model performs *worse than random* ($`1.24\times`$).\

</div>

</div>

<div id="S5.SS6" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">5.6 </span>Failure Modes, Memorization, and Overfitting

<div id="S5.SS6.p1" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S5.SS6.p1.2.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Failure
modes.</span> Diffusion samples exhibit a mild per-channel color tint (a
systematic offset removed by the deployable calibration) and slight
over-sharpening (high-frequency energy $`{\sim}2\times`$ GT standard
deviation at small scale). These are typical small-model artifacts;
neither prevents scene recognition. The direct model’s primary failure
mode is diagnostic blur (see Figure <a href="#A1.F10" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 10 ‣ Appendix A Additional Qualitative Results ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">10</span></a> in the Appendix): regression
to the conditional mean collapses fine structure, rendering vehicles and
lane markings unrecognizable. The jump model’s coarse predictions
(blurry at $`t{+}4`$, increasingly so through the 4-step chain)
represent a related limitation: recognizable multi-second predictions
require stronger generative objectives or higher capacity than explored
here.

</div>

<div id="S5.SS6.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S5.SS6.p2.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Memorization.</span> FID and KID are
computed against *held-out test* frames decoded from ground-truth
latents; the model never sees test scenes during training. We
additionally verify that nearest-neighbor distances from generated
samples to the training manifold do not indicate copying.

</div>

<div id="S5.SS6.p3" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S5.SS6.p3.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Overfitting.</span> Models are compact
(1.6–5.4 M for the world model, 1.7 M for the jump experiment) relative
to the 24,930-frame training set. All results use scene-level held-out
evaluation with 3 random seeds; reported gaps consistently exceed
seed-level variance ($`\text{KID}_{\text{std}}{=}0.005`$ across seeds).
EMA (decay 0.999) provides implicit regularization. The chief threat to
validity is *scale*: our controlled comparisons are meaningful at
compact scale and single front camera (2 Hz), but do not address whether
the same factors hold at GAIA-1 or Cosmos scale, which we state as a
limitation.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S6" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">6 </span>Discussion

<div id="S6.p1" class="ltx_para">

Our experiments reveal a consistent theme: the choice of *evaluation
metric* shapes which model appears superior, and this choice has
practical consequences for AV world model development.

</div>

<div id="S6.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S6.p2.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">The metric matters
more than the architecture.</span> In pooled latent spaces, the DiT
architecture is not the bottleneck (H1 rejected); the prediction
objective and spatial structure are. In VAE latent space, the direct
regressor appears to “win” on every standard metric (CosSim, SSIM, L2)
by collapsing to the conditional mean. Only when we measure distribution
realism via FID and KID does the diffusion model’s $`4.8\times`$
advantage emerge. This suggests that prior AV latent-prediction work
that evaluates exclusively with distortion metrics may underestimate the
quality of generative approaches. We recommend that future AV world
model evaluations report both distortion and distribution metrics,
following the perception-distortion framework of Blau and
Michaeli \[<a href="#bib.bib5" class="ltx_ref">5</a>\].

</div>

<div id="S6.p3" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S6.p3.3.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Controllability as a
differentiator.</span> The diffusion model’s monotonic steering
controllability ($`\rho{=}0.81`$) provides a capability the regression
model lacks entirely ($`\rho{=}{-}0.18`$). This is not merely a
qualitative observation: the inverse-control probe shows the diffusion
model can recover held-out steering commands from predicted futures
($`0.67\times`$ chance error), suggesting the learned latent dynamics
encode action semantics in a way that could support downstream planning.
At larger scale, this property could bridge world models and policy
learning.

</div>

<div id="S6.p4" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S6.p4.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Motion as an
anchoring problem.</span> Our motion analysis reveals that the
single-pass model’s static behavior is not a capacity limitation but a
consequence of the shared-present anchor design. The compact jump model
(1.7 M parameters, $`3\times`$ smaller) recovers motion direction by
re-anchoring on its own predictions, confirming that the architectural
recipe is sound and the bottleneck is the training-inference alignment.
This finding provides a concrete path forward: per-step re-anchoring
combined with stronger temporal supervision (e.g., scheduled sampling or
perceptual losses) should enable recognizable multi-second predictions
as scale increases.

</div>

<div id="S6.p5" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S6.p5.2.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Compact scale as a
feature.</span> While large-scale systems like
GAIA-1 \[<a href="#bib.bib12" class="ltx_ref">12</a>\] (5 B parameters)
and Cosmos \[<a href="#bib.bib14" class="ltx_ref">14</a>\] demonstrate
impressive generation quality, our compact-scale study
($`\sim`$<!-- -->5 M parameters) offers complementary value: controlled
ablations that isolate individual design factors are tractable at this
scale but prohibitively expensive at 5 B. The four ingredients we
identify (spatial tokens, $`x_{0}`$ objective, residual anchoring,
sampling-matched-to-uncertainty) and the deployable calibration strategy
are design principles that transfer to larger systems, and the positive
scaling probe (Section <a href="#S5.SS3" class="ltx_ref"
title="5.3 Perception-Distortion Frontier ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Diffusion Transformer World-Action Model for AV Scene Prediction"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">5.3</span></a>) provides initial evidence
that they do.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S7" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">7 </span>Conclusion

<div id="S7.p1" class="ltx_para">

We built a compact, action-conditioned DiT world model for AV scene
prediction and, in the process, turned two obstacles into results.

</div>

<div id="S7.p2" class="ltx_para">

First, distortion metrics that reward the blurry conditional mean masked
the diffusion model’s quality. By measuring distribution realism via FID
and KID, we revealed a clean perception-distortion frontier where
diffusion is $`4.8\times`$ better on KID, made *deployable* through a
train-derived calibration. This is a metric lesson for AV latent
prediction: world models should be evaluated with distribution metrics,
not distortion alone.

</div>

<div id="S7.p3" class="ltx_para">

Second, single-pass rollouts produced sharp but temporally limited
predictions. We diagnosed the shared-anchor cause and showed that a
compact jump model ($`3\times`$ smaller, re-anchored per step) recovers
forward-motion *direction* on held-out scenes, exceeding the larger
baseline. The model is also action-controllable ($`\rho{=}0.81`$),
providing the planning-relevant property that a regression baseline
lacks.

</div>

<div id="S7.p4" class="ltx_para">

Together these establish a small but controllable AV world model with a
characterized realism frontier and a validated route to coarse motion.

</div>

<div id="S7.p5" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S7.p5.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Future work.</span>
The frontier and the jump result both point the same way: scale
(capacity, data, frame rate) and stronger temporal supervision (e.g.,
perceptual losses, autoregressive scheduled sampling) to convert coarse
motion *direction* into recognizable high-fidelity appearance.
Closed-loop evaluation with predicted (rather than logged) actions and
multi-camera setups are natural extensions. Our controlled findings at
compact scale provide a concrete recipe for the next iteration: the
$`x_{0}`$ objective, residual anchoring, distribution metrics,
deployable calibration, and the jump reparameterization.

</div>

</div>
