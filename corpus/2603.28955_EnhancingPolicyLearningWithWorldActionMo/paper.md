<div id="abstract1" class="ltx_abstract">

###### Abstract

This paper presents the World-Action Model (WAM), an action-regularized
world model that jointly reasons over future visual observations and the
actions that drive state transitions. Unlike conventional world models
trained solely via image prediction, WAM incorporates an inverse
dynamics objective into DreamerV2 that predicts actions from latent
state transitions, encouraging the learned representations to capture
action-relevant structure critical for downstream control. We evaluate
WAM on enhancing policy learning across eight manipulation tasks from
the CALVIN benchmark. We first pretrain a diffusion policy via
behavioral cloning on world model latents, then refine it with
model-based PPO inside the frozen world model. Without modifying the
policy architecture or training procedure, WAM improves average
behavioral cloning success from 59.4% to 71.2% over DreamerV2 and DiWA
baselines. After PPO fine-tuning, WAM achieves 92.8% average success
versus 79.8% for the baseline, with two tasks reaching 100%, using
$`8.7\times`$ fewer training steps.

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">I </span><span id="S1.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_smallcaps">Introduction</span>

<figure id="S1.F1" class="ltx_figure">

world models predict only future observations, treating actions solely
as conditioning inputs. (b) Our World-Action Model adds an inverse

for offline policy fine-tuning via PPO.</span></figcaption>

<div id="S1.p1" class="ltx_para">

World models have become a critical tool for learning environment
dynamics in robotics, allowing agents to plan and refine behaviors
through imagined rollouts rather than expensive real-world
interactions \[<a href="#bib.bib1" class="ltx_ref"
title="Recurrent world models facilitate policy evolution">5</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib2" class="ltx_ref"
title="Learning latent dynamics for planning from pixels">7</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib3" class="ltx_ref"
title="Dream to control: learning behaviors by latent imagination">6</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering atari with discrete world models">8</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib5" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering diverse control tasks through world models">9</a>\].
These models compress high-dimensional observations into compact latent
spaces that capture the temporal and physical structures of
environments, enabling sample-efficient policy learning through imagined
experiences in latent space. Recent work has shown that policies trained
entirely within learned world models can reach strong performance on
robotic manipulation benchmarks \[<a href="#bib.bib6" class="ltx_ref"
title="DITTO: offline imitation learning with world models">4</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib7" class="ltx_ref"
title="LUMOS: language-conditioned imitation learning with world models">12</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref"
title="DiWA: diffusion policy adaptation with world models">3</a>\], and
in some cases transfer directly to physical hardware without further
fine-tuning \[<a href="#bib.bib9" class="ltx_ref"
title="DayDreamer: world models for physical robot learning">17</a>\].

</div>

<div id="S1.p2" class="ltx_para">

However, conventional world models are trained solely to predict future
observations \[<a href="#bib.bib1" class="ltx_ref"
title="Recurrent world models facilitate policy evolution">5</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib2" class="ltx_ref"
title="Learning latent dynamics for planning from pixels">7</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib3" class="ltx_ref"
title="Dream to control: learning behaviors by latent imagination">6</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering atari with discrete world models">8</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib9" class="ltx_ref"
title="DayDreamer: world models for physical robot learning">17</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib7" class="ltx_ref"
title="LUMOS: language-conditioned imitation learning with world models">12</a>\].
They forecast future visual states conditioned on past observations and
actions, but never explicitly model the actions themselves. This
asymmetry limits the quality of learned representations. The world model
encodes each observation into a latent state $`z_{t}`$, which serves as
the direct input to the downstream diffusion policy in model-based
pipelines such as DiWA \[<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref"
title="DiWA: diffusion policy adaptation with world models">3</a>\]—yet
$`z_{t}`$ is optimized only for pixel prediction and KL regularization,
with no explicit pressure to encode action-relevant structure. Recent
work on unified action-world
models \[<a href="#bib.bib14" class="ltx_ref"
title="WorldVLA: towards autoregressive action world model">2</a>\] has
begun to address this gap by jointly generating actions and images
within a single autoregressive architecture, but these approaches rely
on large foundation models and fundamentally redesign the architecture
rather than improving the representations learned by existing world
models.

</div>

<div id="S1.p3" class="ltx_para">

We take a different and complementary approach: rather than redesigning
the architecture, we augment the training objective of
DreamerV2 \[<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering atari with discrete world models">8</a>\] with an
inverse dynamics head that predicts actions from consecutive encoder
embeddings, regularizing the learned representations toward
action-relevant structure. We argue that requiring the model to predict
the action that caused a given state transition forces the encoder to
capture fine-grained information about how the environment responds to
agent behavior. This information is critical for downstream policy
learning but is overlooked when the training signal comes only from
observation reconstruction. This idea draws on inverse dynamics models
in self-supervised representation
learning \[<a href="#bib.bib12" class="ltx_ref"
title="Curiosity-driven exploration by self-supervised prediction">14</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib13" class="ltx_ref"
title="Video pretraining (VPT): learning to act by watching unlabeled online videos">1</a>\],
where predicting actions between consecutive states yields
representations focused on controllable aspects of the environment while
filtering out task-irrelevant distractors.

</div>

<div id="S1.p4" class="ltx_para">

We present the World-Action Model (WAM) and evaluate it on enhancing
policy learning across eight manipulation tasks from the CALVIN
benchmark \[<a href="#bib.bib15" class="ltx_ref"
title="CALVIN: a benchmark for language-conditioned policy learning for long-horizon robot manipulation tasks">11</a>\].
We first pretrain a diffusion policy via behavioral cloning on world
model latents, then refine it with model-based PPO using the frozen
world model as a simulator. Compared to the DiWA
baseline \[<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref"
title="DiWA: diffusion policy adaptation with world models">3</a>\], WAM
improves average behavioral cloning success from 59.4% to 71.2% and
achieves 92.8% average success after PPO fine-tuning versus 79.8%, with
two tasks reaching 100%, using $`8.7\times`$ fewer training steps.

</div>

<div id="S1.p5" class="ltx_para">

Our main contributions are:

- <span id="S1.I1.i1"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S1.I1.i1.p1" class="ltx_para">

  We present WAM, a lightweight extension of DreamerV2 that augments the
  training objective with an inverse dynamics head, explicitly
  regularizing latent representations toward action-relevant structure.

  </div>
- <span id="S1.I1.i2"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S1.I1.i2.p1" class="ltx_para">

  We demonstrate that action regularization improves world model
  generation quality on the CALVIN benchmark. WAM matches or exceeds
  DreamerV2 \[<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref"
  title="Mastering atari with discrete world models">8</a>\] on LPIPS,
  PSNR, SSIM, and FVD with fewer training steps.

  </div>
- <span id="S1.I1.i3"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S1.I1.i3.p1" class="ltx_para">

  We show that the improved representations enhance downstream policy
  learning, outperforming the DiWA baseline on both behavioral cloning
  and PPO fine-tuning across all eight tasks on the CALVIN benchmark.

  </div>

</div>

</div>

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">II </span><span id="S2.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_smallcaps">Related Works</span>

<div id="S2.SS1" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection"><span id="S2.SS1.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">II-A</span> </span><span id="S2.SS1.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">World Models</span>

<div id="S2.SS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

World models learn a predictive representation of environment dynamics,
enabling agents to optimize policies through imagined trajectories
without requiring additional real-world data collection. Ha and
Schmidhuber \[<a href="#bib.bib1" class="ltx_ref"
title="Recurrent world models facilitate policy evolution">5</a>\]
introduced an early approach encoding observations into a latent space
with an RNN for dynamics modeling.
PlaNet \[<a href="#bib.bib2" class="ltx_ref"
title="Learning latent dynamics for planning from pixels">7</a>\]
proposed the Recurrent State-Space Model (RSSM), combining deterministic
and stochastic components for long-horizon prediction. The Dreamer
series \[<a href="#bib.bib3" class="ltx_ref"
title="Dream to control: learning behaviors by latent imagination">6</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering atari with discrete world models">8</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib5" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering diverse control tasks through world models">9</a>\]
progressively extended RSSM-based latent imagination for behavior
learning. DreamerV2 replaced Gaussian latents with categorical
representations, while DreamerV3 introduced robustness techniques that
allowed a single set of hyperparameters to work across diverse domains.
However, these world models are trained purely as observation predictors
without explicitly reasoning about the actions that drive state
transitions. Our work introduces action regularization into the world
model, jointly predicting both actions and future observations to better
capture environment dynamics.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S2.SS2" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection"><span id="S2.SS2.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">II-B</span> </span><span id="S2.SS2.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">World Models for Policy Learning</span>

<div id="S2.SS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

Beyond modeling dynamics, world models can be directly used as
complementary environments for policy optimization, where agents learn
behaviors through imagined rollouts within the learned model. The
Dreamer series \[<a href="#bib.bib3" class="ltx_ref"
title="Dream to control: learning behaviors by latent imagination">6</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering atari with discrete world models">8</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib5" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering diverse control tasks through world models">9</a>\]
first demonstrated that actor-critic policies can be trained entirely
through imagined latent rollouts, and
DayDreamer \[<a href="#bib.bib9" class="ltx_ref"
title="DayDreamer: world models for physical robot learning">17</a>\]
demonstrated this paradigm on physical robots. In offline settings,
DITTO \[<a href="#bib.bib6" class="ltx_ref"
title="DITTO: offline imitation learning with world models">4</a>\]
proposed imitation learning by optimizing a distance metric in a frozen
world model’s latent space, and
LUMOS \[<a href="#bib.bib7" class="ltx_ref"
title="LUMOS: language-conditioned imitation learning with world models">12</a>\]
extended this to language-conditioned multi-task learning.
DiWA \[<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref"
title="DiWA: diffusion policy adaptation with world models">3</a>\]
fine-tuned diffusion policies via PPO within a learned world model,
achieving sample efficiency orders of magnitude beyond model-free
baselines. More recently, WMPO \[<a href="#bib.bib10" class="ltx_ref"
title="WMPO: world model-based policy optimization for vision-language-action models">19</a>\]
introduced pixel-based world models for on-policy GRPO optimization of
Vision-Language-Action models, and
World4RL \[<a href="#bib.bib11" class="ltx_ref"
title="World4RL: diffusion world models for policy refinement with reinforcement learning for robotic manipulation">10</a>\]
employed diffusion-based world models for end-to-end policy refinement.
While these works confirm the effectiveness of world-model-based policy
learning, they rely on world models trained solely for observation
prediction. Our approach enriches the world model with action
prediction, providing a stronger training signal for downstream policy
optimization.

</div>

</div>

</div>

<div id="S3" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">III </span><span id="S3.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_smallcaps">Method</span>

<div id="S3.SS1" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection"><span id="S3.SS1.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">III-A</span> </span><span id="S3.SS1.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">World-Action Model</span>

<div id="S3.SS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

We propose to regularize the world model with an inverse dynamics
objective, producing representations that are simultaneously predictive
of visual dynamics and aware of the actions driving state transitions.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS1.SSS1" class="section ltx_subsubsection">

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection"><span id="S3.SS1.SSS1.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">III-A</span>1 </span><span id="S3.SS1.SSS1.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">World Model Backbone</span>

<div id="S3.SS1.SSS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

WAM builds on the RSSM architecture from
DreamerV2 \[<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering atari with discrete world models">8</a>\]. A
dual-stream CNN encoder processes static and gripper camera images,
fusing them with proprioceptive state to produce embeddings
$`e_{t}\in\mathbb{R}^{1554}`$. The RSSM models latent dynamics through:

|  |  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|----|
|  | $`\displaystyle h_{t}`$ | $`\displaystyle=f_{\phi}(h_{t-1},z_{t-1},a_{t-1})`$ |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(1)</span> |
|  | $`\displaystyle z_{t}`$ | $`\displaystyle\sim q_{\phi}(z_{t}\mid h_{t},e_{t})`$ |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(2)</span> |
|  | $`\displaystyle\hat{z}_{t}`$ | $`\displaystyle\sim p_{\phi}(z_{t}\mid h_{t})`$ |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(3)</span> |
|  | $`\displaystyle\hat{o}_{t}`$ | $`\displaystyle\sim p_{\phi}(o_{t}\mid f_{t}),`$ |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(4)</span> |

where $`h_{t}`$ is the deterministic recurrent state, $`z_{t}`$ is a
stochastic categorical variable (32$`\times`$<!-- -->32), and
$`f_{t}=[h_{t};z_{t}]\in\mathbb{R}^{2048}`$ is the combined latent
feature used for decoding and policy learning.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S3.SS1.SSS2" class="section ltx_subsubsection">

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection"><span id="S3.SS1.SSS2.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">III-A</span>2 </span><span id="S3.SS1.SSS2.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Action-Regularized World Model</span>

<div id="S3.SS1.SSS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

The observation-only objective (Eq. <a href="#S3.E4" class="ltx_ref"
title="In III-A1 World Model Backbone ‣ III-A World-Action Model ‣ III Method ‣ Enhancing Policy Learning with World-Action Model"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">4</span></a>) produces features optimized
for pixel reconstruction, potentially discarding action-relevant
information. WAM addresses this by augmenting the world model with an
inverse dynamics component, yielding a unified model
$`\mathcal{M}_{\theta}`$ with two pathways:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\hat{o}_{t}=\mathcal{M}_{\theta}^{\text{world}}(o_{1:t},\,a_{1:t-1}),\quad\hat{a}_{t}=\mathcal{M}_{\theta}^{\text{action}}(e_{t},\,e_{t+1}),
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(5)</span> |

where $`\mathcal{M}_{\theta}^{\text{world}}`$ denotes the RSSM pathway
(Eqs. <a href="#S3.E1" class="ltx_ref"
title="In III-A1 World Model Backbone ‣ III-A World-Action Model ‣ III Method ‣ Enhancing Policy Learning with World-Action Model"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">1</span></a>–<a href="#S3.E4" class="ltx_ref"
title="In III-A1 World Model Backbone ‣ III-A World-Action Model ‣ III Method ‣ Enhancing Policy Learning with World-Action Model"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">4</span></a>) and
$`\mathcal{M}_{\theta}^{\text{action}}`$ is an inverse dynamics head:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\hat{a}_{t}=\psi([e_{t};\,e_{t+1}]),
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(6)</span> |

with $`\psi`$ a three-layer MLP and $`[\cdot\,;\,\cdot]`$ denoting
concatenation. A critical design in WAM is the cascading effect created
by the action head through the full model. We operate on encoder
embeddings $`e_{t}`$ rather than RSSM features $`f_{t}`$, since
$`f_{t}`$ receives $`a_{t-1}`$ through the GRU
(Eq. <a href="#S3.E1" class="ltx_ref"
title="In III-A1 World Model Backbone ‣ III-A World-Action Model ‣ III Method ‣ Enhancing Policy Learning with World-Action Model"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">1</span></a>) and would make action
prediction trivially solvable. By regularizing the encoder directly, the
action-aware structure in $`e_{t}`$ cascades forward: it shapes the
posterior $`z_{t}\sim q_{\phi}(z_{t}\mid h_{t},e_{t})`$, which the KL
loss propagates to the prior
$`\hat{z}_{t}\sim p_{\phi}(z_{t}\mid h_{t})`$, and ultimately reaches
the diffusion policy through imagined rollouts generated from
$`\hat{z}_{t}`$. This chain ensures that the action prediction signal at
the encoder level translates into more informative features for
downstream control. As illustrated in
Fig. <a href="#S3.F2" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 2 ‣ III-A2 Action-Regularized World Model ‣ III-A World-Action Model ‣ III Method ‣ Enhancing Policy Learning with World-Action Model"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">2</span></a>, the action-aware structure
cascades from the encoder through the posterior to the prior across
timesteps.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS1.SSS2.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S3.SS1.SSS2.p2.4.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Training
Objective.</span> WAM is trained end-to-end by minimizing:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\mathcal{L}_{\text{{WAM}}}=\lambda_{\text{KL}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}}+\lambda_{\text{img}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}+\lambda_{\text{act}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{action}},
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(7)</span> |

where
$`\mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}}=\text{KL}\big[q_{\phi}(z_{t}\mid h_{t},e_{t})\,\|\,p_{\phi}(z_{t}\mid h_{t})\big]`$,
$`\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}=\|o_{t}-\hat{o}_{t}\|_{2}^{2}`$, and
$`\mathcal{L}_{\text{action}}=\|\hat{a}_{t}-a_{t}\|_{1}`$. We carefully
tune the loss coefficients to balance the trade-off between
reconstruction quality and action prediction, ensuring that the encoder
learns representations that serve both objectives effectively.

</div>

</div>

</div>

<div id="S3.SS2" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection"><span id="S3.SS2.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">III-B</span> </span><span id="S3.SS2.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">Enhancing Downstream Policy Learning with WAM</span>

<div id="S3.SS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

After world model training, we leverage WAM to enhance downstream policy
learning. The frozen WAM defines a latent space MDP
$`\mathcal{M}_{\text{wm}}=(\mathcal{Z},\mathcal{A},P_{\phi},R_{\psi},\gamma)`$,
where $`\mathcal{Z}`$ is WAM’s latent space, $`P_{\phi}`$ the learned
dynamics, and $`R_{\psi}`$ a learned reward classifier. We extract
features $`f_{t}\in\mathbb{R}^{2048}`$ from expert demonstrations
$`\mathcal{D}_{\text{exp}}`$ (50 episodes per task) and train a
diffusion-based policy in two stages: behavioral cloning followed by
optional offline RL fine-tuning.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS2.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S3.SS2.p2.4.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Behavioral
Cloning.</span> We freeze the WAM and extract features
$`f_{t}\in\mathbb{R}^{2048}`$ from expert demonstrations
$`\mathcal{D}_{\text{exp}}`$. Following
DiWA \[<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref"
title="DiWA: diffusion policy adaptation with world models">3</a>\], we
adopt a DiffusionMLP policy $`\pi_{\theta}(a_{t}\mid f_{t})`$ that
generates actions by iteratively denoising Gaussian noise through $`K`$
steps:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
a_{t}^{k-1}=\mu_{\theta}(f_{t},a_{t}^{k},k)+\sigma_{k}\epsilon,\quad\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I),
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(8)</span> |

where $`a_{t}^{K}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)`$ and $`a_{t}^{0}`$ are the
predicted actions. The policy is trained by minimizing the denoising
objective:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}}=\mathbb{E}_{k,\epsilon,(f_{t},a_{t})}\left[\|\mu_{\theta}(f_{t},a_{t}^{k},k)-a_{t}^{k-1}\|^{2}\right].
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(9)</span> |

The key advantage of WAM is the cascading effect: the action-regularized
encoder produces action-aware $`e_{t}`$, which conditions the posterior
$`z_{t}\sim q_{\phi}(z_{t}\mid h_{t},e_{t})`$, yielding latent features
$`f_{t}=[h_{t};z_{t}]`$ that encode richer action-relevant information.
The diffusion policy trained on these features directly benefits from
this improved representation.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS2.p3" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S3.SS2.p3.4.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Offline Policy
Fine-tuning.</span> After behavioral cloning, we further improve the
policy using DPPO \[<a href="#bib.bib19" class="ltx_ref"
title="Diffusion policy policy optimization">15</a>\] entirely within
the frozen world model’s latent space, requiring no physical
interactions. During imagined rollouts, observations are unavailable, so
the model relies on the prior
$`\hat{z}_{t}\sim p_{\phi}(z_{t}\mid h_{t})`$ to generate latent states.
The cascading effect of WAM is critical here: the action-regularized
encoder shapes the posterior $`z_{t}`$ during training, and the KL loss
propagates this action-aware structure to the prior $`\hat{z}_{t}`$,
ensuring that imagined rollouts benefit from action-relevant
representations. At each iteration, 50 parallel imagined rollouts are
generated within $`\mathcal{M}_{\text{wm}}`$ using the current policy.
The fine-tuning objective maximizes:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\theta^{*}=\arg\max_{\theta}\;\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi_{\theta},P_{\phi}}\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T}\gamma^{t}R_{\psi}(z_{t},a_{t})\right],
``` |  | <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_equation ltx_align_right">(10)</span> |

where $`R_{\psi}`$ is a binary reward classifier. Since WAM produces
different latent representations than the baseline DreamerV2, we retrain
the reward classifiers on WAM features following DiWA’s
pipeline \[<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref"
title="DiWA: diffusion policy adaptation with world models">3</a>\]. All
8 classifiers achieve $`{\geq}`$<!-- -->0.97 precision and 1.00 recall,
confirming reliable reward estimation in WAM’s latent space. The policy
is updated via clipped PPO. During rollouts, the diffusion policy uses
10 denoising steps with BC regularization
($`\alpha_{\text{BC}}\!=\!0.025`$) to prevent catastrophic forgetting.
As illustrated in Fig. <a href="#S3.F2" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 2 ‣ III-A2 Action-Regularized World Model ‣ III-A World-Action Model ‣ III Method ‣ Enhancing Policy Learning with World-Action Model"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">2</span></a>, the action-aware structure
propagates from the encoder through the prior to the imagined rollouts,
benefiting both policy optimization and reward estimation during offline
adaptation.

</div>

</div>

</div>

<div id="S4.SS1" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection"><span id="S4.SS1.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">IV-A</span> </span><span id="S4.SS1.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">Action Regularization Enhances WAM.</span>

<div id="S4.SS1.SSS1" class="section ltx_subsubsection">

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection"><span id="S4.SS1.SSS1.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">IV-A</span>1 </span><span id="S4.SS1.SSS1.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Baselines</span>

<div id="S4.SS1.SSS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

We assess generation quality by comparing WAM against the baseline world
model DreamerV2 \[<a href="#bib.bib16" class="ltx_ref"
title="DreamingV2: reinforcement learning with discrete world models without reconstruction">13</a>\].
We sample 100 random sequences from the CALVIN validation set and
perform 50-step open-loop imagination rollouts, conditioned on the first
ground-truth observation and using ground-truth actions. We report PSNR,
SSIM, LPIPS \[<a href="#bib.bib17" class="ltx_ref"
title="The unreasonable effectiveness of deep features as a perceptual metric">18</a>\],
and FVD \[<a href="#bib.bib18" class="ltx_ref"
title="Towards accurate generative models of video: a new metric &amp; challenges">16</a>\]
to evaluate generation quality.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S4.SS1.SSS2" class="section ltx_subsubsection">

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection"><span id="S4.SS1.SSS2.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">IV-A</span>2 </span><span id="S4.SS1.SSS2.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Training Settings</span>

<div id="S4.SS1.SSS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

We use a sequence length of $`T=50`$, a batch size of 500, and a
learning rate of $`3\times 10^{-4}`$ with AdamW (weight decay $`0.05`$).
The KL balance coefficient is set to $`\alpha=0.8`$, with loss weights
$`\lambda_{\text{KL}}=3.0`$, $`\lambda_{\text{img}}=1.0`$, and
$`\lambda_{\text{act}}=1000.0`$. The action prediction loss uses L1
regression. WAM is trained for only 230K gradient steps on
$`\sim`$<!-- -->512K play frames from the CALVIN dataset, roughly
$`8.7\times`$ fewer than the 2M steps used by the baseline DreamingV2,
while still achieving stronger downstream policy performance.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

Following the DiWA pipeline, we first pretrain the diffusion policy via
behavioral cloning. Both DiWA and our method use the same frozen encoder
and RSSM to extract $`f_{t}\in\mathbb{R}^{2048}`$ features from 50
expert demonstrations per task. The downstream policy is a DiffusionMLP
with $`K\!=\!20`$ denoising steps and action horizon $`T_{a}\!=\!4`$,
trained for 5,000 epochs with a batch size of 256, a learning rate of
$`10^{-4}`$ (cosine decay to $`10^{-5}`$), weight decay of $`10^{-6}`$,
and EMA with a decay of 0.995. The only difference between the two
conditions is which world model provides the features.

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS3" class="section ltx_subsubsection">

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection"><span id="S4.SS2.SSS3.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">IV-B</span>3 </span><span id="S4.SS2.SSS3.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Online Policy Fine-tuning Settings</span>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS3.p1" class="ltx_para">

Following DiWA \[<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref"
title="DiWA: diffusion policy adaptation with world models">3</a>\], we
fine-tune the BC-pretrained diffusion policies using
DPPO \[<a href="#bib.bib19" class="ltx_ref"
title="Diffusion policy policy optimization">15</a>\] entirely within
the frozen world model’s latent space, requiring no physical
interactions during policy optimization. At each iteration, the current
policy generates 50 parallel imagined rollouts within the frozen world
model’s latent space. The policy is updated via a clipped PPO surrogate
objective with a batch size of 7,500, 10 update epochs per iteration,
actor learning rate $`10^{-5}`$, critic learning rate $`10^{-3}`$,
$`\gamma=0.999`$, and GAE $`\lambda=0.95`$. During rollouts, the
diffusion policy uses 10 denoising steps with BC regularization
($`\alpha_{\text{BC}}=0.025`$) to prevent catastrophic forgetting.
Fine-tuning runs for 800 iterations with evaluation every 25 iterations.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS3.p2" class="ltx_para">

Crucially, since the WAM encoder produces different latent
representations than the baseline DreamerV2, we retrain the reward
classifiers on WAM features following DiWA’s full pipeline:
(1) featurize the entire CALVIN play dataset with the WAM encoder,
(2) extract matched features and raw states from the same 50 expert
episodes per task (seed 42), (3) generate ground-truth reward labels by
replaying expert actions in the CALVIN simulator, (4) augment training
data with imagined rollouts inside the WAM, (5) balance class
distribution, and (6) train contrastive reward classifiers. All 8
classifiers achieve $`{\geq}`$<!-- -->0.97 precision and 1.00 recall on
training data, confirming reliable reward estimation in WAM’s latent
space.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS4" class="section ltx_subsubsection">

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection"><span id="S4.SS2.SSS4.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">IV-B</span>4 </span><span id="S4.SS2.SSS4.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Behavioral Cloning Results</span>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS4.p1" class="ltx_para">

Each policy is evaluated on 29 held-out initial configurations per task.
The frozen world model encodes the current observation into $`f_{t}`$,
and the policy generates a 4-step action chunk via DDPM sampling (up to
18 decision points per episode, 72 max steps). An episode succeeds if
CALVIN’s built-in task checker confirms completion.
Table <a href="#S4.T3" class="ltx_ref"
title="TABLE III ‣ IV-B1 Baselines ‣ IV-B Enhancing Policy Learning with WAM ‣ IV Experiments ‣ Enhancing Policy Learning with World-Action Model"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">III</span></a> reports results across all
eight CALVIN tasks. WAM outperforms the DiWA baseline on 7 out of 8
tasks, achieving an average success rate of 61.7% compared to 45.8%. The
largest gains appear on tasks involving articulated objects:
<span id="S4.SS2.SSS4.p1.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">close_drawer</span> (+31.1pp),
<span id="S4.SS2.SSS4.p1.1.2"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">move_slider_right</span> (+31.1pp), and
<span id="S4.SS2.SSS4.p1.1.3"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">open_drawer</span> (+20.0pp), where
precise position control is critical, and the encoder must capture
fine-grained spatial cues that distinguish successful manipulation
trajectories. The only task where the baseline marginally outperforms
WAM is <span id="S4.SS2.SSS4.p1.1.4"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">turn_on_led</span> (44.8% vs. 41.4%),
which we attribute to evaluation variance given the small number of test
episodes. These results confirm that by jointly training on action
prediction and state prediction, WAM learns representations that are
more informative for downstream imitation learning without any
modification to the policy architecture or training procedure.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS4.p2" class="ltx_para">

Figure <a href="#S4.F4" class="ltx_ref"
title="Figure 4 ‣ IV-B4 Behavioral Cloning Results ‣ IV-B Enhancing Policy Learning with WAM ‣ IV Experiments ‣ Enhancing Policy Learning with World-Action Model"><span

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection"><span id="S4.SS2.SSS5.5.1.1" class="ltx_text">IV-B</span>5 </span><span id="S4.SS2.SSS5.6.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Diffusion Policy Fine-tuning Results</span>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS5.p1" class="ltx_para">

Table <a href="#S4.T4" class="ltx_ref"
title="TABLE IV ‣ IV-B2 Behavioral Cloning Training Settings ‣ IV-B Enhancing Policy Learning with WAM ‣ IV Experiments ‣ Enhancing Policy Learning with World-Action Model"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">IV</span></a> reports the results. After
800 iterations of fine-tuning, WAM reaches a 92.8% average success rate
across all eight tasks, outperforming DiWA (79.8%) by 13.0 percentage
points. WAM outperforms DiWA on every task, with
<span id="S4.SS2.SSS5.p1.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">turn_on_lightbulb</span> and
<span id="S4.SS2.SSS5.p1.1.2"

class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">turn_on_led</span> (96.6% vs. 86.2%),
suggesting that action-regularized representations provide a better
optimization landscape for policy fine-tuning within the world model.
Combined with the BC results, these findings demonstrate that WAM
features benefit both offline imitation and online policy adaptation.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS5.p2" class="ltx_para">

These results show that jointly training on action and state prediction
not only improves BC policy quality (61.7% vs. 45.8% average) but also
provides a stronger starting point for model-based RL fine-tuning (92.8%
vs. 79.8% average), yielding a 13.0 percentage point gain over DiWA with
the same policy architecture and $`8.7\times`$ fewer world model
training steps.

<div id="S5" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">V </span><span id="S5.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_smallcaps">Conclusion</span>

<div id="S5.p1" class="ltx_para">

We presented the World-Action Model (WAM), a world model augmented with
an inverse dynamics objective that regularizes learned representations
toward action-relevant structure. The key insight is that under standard
reconstruction objectives, the latent state $`z_{t}`$ fed to the
downstream diffusion policy is never explicitly trained to be
action-informative. By jointly predicting actions and future states, WAM
propagates action-relevant gradients through the representation pathway,
improving the quality of $`z_{t}`$ for downstream control. Experiments
on eight CALVIN manipulation tasks show that WAM improves behavioral
cloning success from 45.8% to 61.7% and model-based RL fine-tuning from
79.8% to 92.8%, while using $`8.7\times`$ fewer world model training
steps than the baseline.

</div>
