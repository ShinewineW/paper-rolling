###### Abstract

Recent advances in generative AI have spurred the development of world
models capable of simulating 3D-consistent environments and interactions
with static objects. A significant limitation of these models is the
ability to model dynamic, reactive agents which can intelligently
influence and interact with the world. We introduce COMBAT, a real-time,
action-controlled world model trained on the complex 1v1 fighting game
Tekken 3 to address these shortcomings. Our work demonstrates that
diffusion models can successfully simulate a dynamic opponent that
reacts to player actions while learning its behavior implicitly.

Our approach utilizes a 1.2 billion parameter Diffusion Transformer,
conditioned on latent representations from a deep compression
autoencoder. We employ state-of-the-art techniques, including causal
distillation and diffusion forcing to achieve real-time inference.
Crucially, we observe the emergence of sophisticated agent behavior by
training the model solely on single-player inputs, without any explicit
supervision for the opponent’s policy. Unlike traditional imitation
learning methods which require complete action labels, COMBAT learns
effectively from partially observed data to generate responsive
behaviors for a controllable primary player (Player 1). We present our
results from an extensive study and introduce novel evaluation methods
to benchmark this emergent agent behavior. In the process, establishing
a strong foundation for training interactive agents within
diffusion-based world models.

</div>

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">1 </span>Introduction

<div id="S1.p1" class="ltx_para">

As the fidelity of video generation methods improve with increased
understanding of real-world phenomena and context, interactive world
models trained on gameplay and real-world data have emerged to
accelerate these advances \[<a href="#bib.bib19" class="ltx_ref"
title="Video generation models as world simulators">4</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref"
title="Genie: generative interactive environments">5</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib1" class="ltx_ref"
title="Diffusion models are real-time game engines">24</a>\]. Generating
spatially and temporally consistent world simulations are the primary
focus. Yet, in real-world scenarios, the most unpredictable components
are reactive agents that can observe, plan, and influence their
environment. This is especially evident in autonomous driving,
navigation, and combat scenarios.

</div>

<div id="S1.p2" class="ltx_para">

Recent work demonstrates that autoregressive diffusion models are very
effective for world simulation. Recent advances make these models
real-time through distribution matching distillation (DMD)
\[<a href="#bib.bib20" class="ltx_ref"
title="Improved distribution matching distillation for fast image synthesis">27</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib2" class="ltx_ref"
title="From slow bidirectional to fast autoregressive video diffusion models">28</a>\]
and diffusion forcing \[<a href="#bib.bib22" class="ltx_ref"
title="Self forcing: bridging the train-test gap in autoregressive video diffusion">15</a>\]
to overcome autoregressive drift. This work has enabled neural game
simulations for first-person games such as Minecraft and CS:GO
\[<a href="#bib.bib25" class="ltx_ref"
title="Hunyuan-gamecraft: high-dynamic interactive game video generation with hybrid history condition">18</a>\]
and showcase excellent causal understanding of actions and their effects
on generated frames.

</div>

<div id="S1.p3" class="ltx_para">

However, real-world and game environments also contain rich information
about how agents (e.g. humans, NPCs and autonomous systems) respond to
environmental dynamics. Current methods could greatly benefit from
learning agent behavior from this observational data, but the partial
observability and unstructured nature poses significant challenges. For
example, while we might observe a pedestrian changing pathing to avoid a
vehicle; the exact observations and decision processes of the human
agent remain hidden.

</div>

<div id="S1.p4" class="ltx_para">

We present COMBAT (<span id="S1.p4.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">C</span>onditional world
<span id="S1.p4.1.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">M</span>odel for
<span id="S1.p4.1.3" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">B</span>ehavioral
<span id="S1.p4.1.4" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">A</span>gent
<span id="S1.p4.1.5" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">T</span>raining), an
interactive world model that learns underlying agent behavior and
movement dynamics directly from partially observed multi-agent systems.
By training a world model on Tekken 3 gameplay with conditioning only on
Player 1’s input, we observe emergent tactical behavior in Player 2
without explicit behavioral supervision. We select Tekken 3 as it
provides an ideal controlled environment with clear visual feedback,
deterministic game mechanics, diverse movesets, and frame-precise timing
requirements.

</div>

<div id="S1.p5" class="ltx_para">

Our approach uses a 1.2B parameter diffusion transformer trained on 1.2M
frames across 1,000 gameplay rounds. We first train a Deep Compression
AutoEncoder (DCAE) \[<a href="#bib.bib7" class="ltx_ref"
title="Deep compression autoencoder for efficient high-resolution diffusion models">8</a>\]
to obtain highly compressed latent representations, then train the world
model to generate temporally consistent gameplay sequences. COMBAT
successfully learns to control Player 1 from conditioning signals, while
Player 2 emerges with realistic combat behaviors including blocking,
counterattacking, and combo execution. Through decoder distillation and
CausVid DMD \[<a href="#bib.bib2" class="ltx_ref"
title="From slow bidirectional to fast autoregressive video diffusion models">28</a>\]
techniques, we achieve real-time generation at interactive frame rates.

</div>

<div id="S1.p6" class="ltx_para">

We introduce novel benchmarking methods to evaluate emergent agent
behavior. This includes measurement of behavioral diversity and tactical
understanding. Our extensive analysis demonstrates that world models can
serve as a new paradigm for learning agent behaviors from observational
data, with implications for multi-agent AI systems beyond gaming.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S2" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">2 </span>Related Work

<div id="S2.SS1" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">2.1 </span>Video Diffusion Models

<div id="S2.SS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

The remarkable success of diffusion models in image synthesis
\[<a href="#bib.bib14" class="ltx_ref"
title="Zero-shot text-to-image generation">20</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib13" class="ltx_ref"
title="High-resolution image synthesis with latent diffusion models">21</a>\]
has naturally inspired their extension to video generation. Early
approaches adapted U-Net architectures from image models, achieving
results in short-form video synthesis
\[<a href="#bib.bib15" class="ltx_ref"
title="Stable video diffusion: scaling latent video diffusion models to large datasets">3</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib16" class="ltx_ref"
title="AnimateDiff: animate your personalized text-to-image diffusion models without specific tuning">10</a>\].
However, the convolutional nature of U-Net presents challenges for
video: it struggles to capture long-range temporal dependencies and
scales poorly with sequence length, often leading to temporal
incoherence. Our work is positioned at the intersection of generative
world models, video diffusion architectures, and behavioral modeling. We
review key advancements in these areas to contextualize our
contribution.

</div>

<div id="S2.SS1.p2" class="ltx_para">

To address these limitations, Transformer-based video models have
emerged. Peebles et al. \[<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref"
title="Scalable diffusion models with transformers">19</a>\]
demonstrates that Diffusion Transformers (DiT) could surpass U-Nets with
respect to image generation with superior scaling properties. Subsequent
work has applied this architecture to video. Models such as W.A.L.T
\[<a href="#bib.bib18" class="ltx_ref"
title="Photorealistic video generation with diffusion models">11</a>\]
and CogVideoX \[<a href="#bib.bib17" class="ltx_ref"
title="CogVideoX: text-to-video diffusion models with an expert transformer">26</a>\]
show that DiT self-attention mechanisms effectively model complex
spatiotemporal relationships in video data, enabling longer, more
coherent sequences. Our work builds on this foundation, employing a DiT
backbone tailored for action-conditioned dynamics in interactive
environments.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S2.SS2" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">2.2 </span>Neural Game Engines and World Models

<div id="S2.SS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

Recent advances demonstrate that generative models can serve as
complete, neural game engines, replacing traditional rendering and state
update logic. As an example, GameGAN learns to imitate 2D games from raw
pixels and actions using GANs with explicit memory modules
\[<a href="#bib.bib26" class="ltx_ref"
title="Learning to Simulate Dynamic Environments with GameGAN">17</a>\].
More recently, diffusion transformers have become dominant for this
task.

</div>

<div id="S2.SS2.p2" class="ltx_para">

GameNGen is another example of a fully neural DOOM engine that generates
frames conditioned on past frames and actions enabling real-time
simulation \[<a href="#bib.bib1" class="ltx_ref"
title="Diffusion models are real-time game engines">24</a>\]. DIAMOND
trains diffusion-based world models achieving state-of-the-art RL
performance while producing playable Counter-Strike simulations
\[<a href="#bib.bib3" class="ltx_ref"
title="Diffusion for world modeling: visual details matter in atari">1</a>\].
GameGen-X extends this, training on million-clip datasets to enable
long-horizon, interactive open-world gameplay
\[<a href="#bib.bib23" class="ltx_ref"
title="GameGen-x: interactive open-world game video generation">6</a>\].

</div>

<div id="S2.SS2.p3" class="ltx_para">

These methods validate that neural models can learn complex game
dynamics from observational data. Our work adopts similar architectural
foundations but introduces a novel objective: modeling emergent behavior
of uncontrolled opponents that arises solely from conditioning on
controllable player actions.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S2.SS3" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">2.3 </span>Multi-Modal and Behavioral World Models

<div id="S2.SS3.p1" class="ltx_para">

While traditional world models focus on visual prediction, recent work
has enabled greater fidelity and behavioral learning. Our work adopts
joint RGB-pose representation to enforce structural consistency in
character movements.

</div>

<div id="S2.SS3.p2" class="ltx_para">

In parallel, learning agent behavior within world models has
predominantly followed two paths. The first is <span id="S2.SS3.p2.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">model-based reinforcement
learning</span>, where an agent’s policy is trained using a learned
dynamics model and an extrinsic reward signal. Works like
<span id="S2.SS3.p2.1.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">DreamerV3</span>
exemplify this, achieving mastery in diverse domains by learning
behaviors entirely within the latent space of a world model
\[<a href="#bib.bib27" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering diverse domains through world models">12</a>\]. The
second path is <span id="S2.SS3.p2.1.3"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">imitation learning</span>, which learns
policies from expert demonstrations. Methods like
<span id="S2.SS3.p2.1.4" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Generative
Adversarial Imitation Learning (GAIL)</span> require explicit
state-action supervision for all agents to mimic expert behavior
\[<a href="#bib.bib24" class="ltx_ref"
title="Generative adversarial imitation learning">14</a>\].

</div>

<div id="S2.SS3.p3" class="ltx_para">

Our approach diverges from both paradigms. We demonstrate that complex,
reactive multi-agent behaviors emerge implicitly as a property of world
modeling itself, without engineered reward signals and using only
partially observed data where just one agent’s actions are provided as a
condition.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S2.SS4" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">2.4 </span>Optimization Techniques for Interactive Generation

<div id="S2.SS4.p1" class="ltx_para">

Real-time interactive generation requires addressing both architectural
efficiency and sampling speed. Recent advances in attention mechanisms
include FlexAttention \[<a href="#bib.bib29" class="ltx_ref"
title="Flex attention: a programming model for generating optimized attention kernels">9</a>\],
which enables flexible attention patterns, and Longformer
\[<a href="#bib.bib28" class="ltx_ref"
title="Longformer: the long-document transformer">2</a>\], which
combines local sliding-window attention with global context. We
incorporate local-global attention patterns inspired by these works to
balance efficiency with temporal coverage.

</div>

<div id="S2.SS4.p2" class="ltx_para">

For sampling efficiency, Distribution Matching Distillation (DMD)
\[<a href="#bib.bib20" class="ltx_ref"
title="Improved distribution matching distillation for fast image synthesis">27</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib2" class="ltx_ref"
title="From slow bidirectional to fast autoregressive video diffusion models">28</a>\]
and diffusion forcing \[<a href="#bib.bib22" class="ltx_ref"
title="Self forcing: bridging the train-test gap in autoregressive video diffusion">15</a>\]
have proven effective techniques for reducing sampling steps while
mitigating autoregressive drift. These techniques enable real-time
neural simulation for complex games
\[<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref"
title="Genie: generative interactive environments">5</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib1" class="ltx_ref"
title="Diffusion models are real-time game engines">24</a>\]. We adapt
DMD through CausVid distillation to achieve interactive frame rates
while preserving behavioral quality.

</div>

<div id="S2.SS4.p3" class="ltx_para">

The Muon optimizer \[<a href="#bib.bib11" class="ltx_ref"
title="Muon: an optimizer for hidden layers in neural networks">16</a>\]
introduces orthogonalization into momentum-based updates, improving
conditioning of weight updates and outperforming AdamW in training speed
benchmarks. We incorporate Muon optimization to enhance training
efficiency of our large-scale diffusion transformers.

</div>

</div>

</div>

<div id="S3" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">3 </span>Method

<div id="S3.p1" class="ltx_para">

Our proposed and studied approach, <span id="S3.p1.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">COMBAT</span>, learns to simulate a
complex, multi-agent environment by training a generative world model on
video observations. World models have shown promise in mastering diverse
domains \[<a href="#bib.bib27" class="ltx_ref"
title="Mastering diverse domains through world models">12</a>\] and
creating interactive environments \[<a href="#bib.bib8" class="ltx_ref"
title="Genie: generative interactive environments">5</a>,
<a href="#bib.bib9" class="ltx_ref"
title="Learning interactive real-world simulators">25</a>\]. We extend
this paradigm to a competitive fighting game, where the model must learn
the opponent’s behavior without explicit action labels.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS1" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">3.1 </span>Problem Formulation

<div id="S3.SS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

The task is as follows: Primarily, learning a conditional video
generation model that implicitly captures an opponent’s policy. We
select the fighting game *Tekken 3* as our environment for three key
reasons:

1.  <span id="S3.I1.i1"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">1.</span></span>
    <div id="S3.I1.i1.p1" class="ltx_para">

    <span id="S3.I1.i1.p1.2.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Bounded
    Temporal Dependency</span>: The game state is largely Markovian,
    where

    |     |                                                            |     |
    |-----|------------------------------------------------------------|-----|
    |     |                                                            
           ``` math                                                    
           P(s_{t+1}\mid s_{\leq t})\approx P(s_{t+1}\mid s_{t-k:t}),  
           ```                                                         |     |

    for a small history window $`k`$, since all relevant information is
    contained within recent frames.

    </div>
2.  <span id="S3.I1.i2"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">2.</span></span>
    <div id="S3.I1.i2.p1" class="ltx_para">

    <span id="S3.I1.i2.p1.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Rich
    Action Space</span>: Characters possess diverse movesets, with over
    40 unique actions and complex combos, providing a challenging domain
    for behavior modeling.

    </div>
3.  <span id="S3.I1.i3"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">3.</span></span>
    <div id="S3.I1.i3.p1" class="ltx_para">

    <span id="S3.I1.i3.p1.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Strategic
    Depth</span>: Success requires a blend of rapid reactions and
    long-term tactical planning.

    </div>

</div>

<div id="S3.SS1.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S3.SS1.p2.4.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Formal Problem
Statement:</span> Given a dataset of partially observed multi-agent
trajectories

|     |                                              |     |
|-----|----------------------------------------------|-----|
|     |                                              
       ``` math                                      
       D=\{(s_{t},a_{t}^{(1)},s_{t+1})\}_{t=1}^{T},  
       ```                                           |     |

where $`s_{t}\in\mathbb{R}^{H\times W\times 3}`$ is a game frame and
$`a_{t}^{(1)}\in\{0,1\}^{8}`$ is the observed multi-hot input for
Player 1. The actions of Player 2, $`a_{t}^{(2)}`$, remain unobserved.
Our objective is to learn a conditional world model

|     |                                                   |     |
|-----|---------------------------------------------------|-----|
|     |                                                   
       ``` math                                           
       P_{\theta}(s_{t+1}\mid s_{t-k:t},a_{t-k:t}^{(1)})  
       ```                                                |     |

that can accurately predict subsequent frames.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS1.p3" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S3.SS1.p3.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Key
Innovation:</span> Unlike traditional imitation learning methods that
require explicit action supervision for all
agents \[<a href="#bib.bib24" class="ltx_ref"
title="Generative adversarial imitation learning">14</a>\], COMBAT is
trained without Player 2’s action labels. The model must infer Player
2’s policy,

|     |                                               |     |
|-----|-----------------------------------------------|-----|
|     |                                               
       ``` math                                       
       \pi^{(2)}(a_{t}^{(2)}\mid s_{t},a_{t}^{(1)}),  
       ```                                            |     |

as an emergent property of generating temporally consistent and
plausible multi-agent interactions. This forces the world model to learn
reactive and strategic opponent behavior implicitly.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS2" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">3.2 </span>Tekken 3 Gameplay Dataset

<div id="S3.SS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

To train our model, we collect a large-scale dataset of *Tekken 3*
gameplay, totaling 1,000 rounds (approximately 7 hours or 1.2 million
frames). The dataset features a variety of characters and a balanced
win–loss ratio between the two players. For each frame, captured at a
resolution of $`3\times 448\times 736`$, we provide synchronized
annotations including: a) action inputs for both players, b) health and
timer status, c) 68-point body pose coordinates, and d) player
segmentation masks. Our data collection and annotation pipeline will be
made publicly available in conjunction with the publication of this
paper.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S3.SS3" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">3.3 </span>Model Architecture

<div id="S3.SS3.p1" class="ltx_para">

Our world model architecture integrates three main components:

- <span id="S3.I2.i1"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S3.I2.i1.p1" class="ltx_para">

  a multi-modal variational autoencoder for high-ratio state
  compression,

  </div>
- <span id="S3.I2.i2"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S3.I2.i2.p1" class="ltx_para">

  an embedding module for player actions and diffusion timesteps, and

  </div>
- <span id="S3.I2.i3"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S3.I2.i3.p1" class="ltx_para">

  a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) backbone for autoregressive prediction
  in the latent space.

  </div>

</div>

<div id="S3.SS3.p2" class="ltx_para">

We train two versions of the model: one using only RGB latents and
another using a joint visual–pose latent representation.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS3.SSS1" class="section ltx_subsubsection">

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection">3.3.1 </span>Multi-Modal Latent Encoding

<div id="S3.SS3.SSS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

To create an efficient latent representation, we first train a
340M-parameter joint RGB–pose variational autoencoder. This model learns
a shared embedding space by compressing concatenated visual frames
($`3\times 448\times 736`$) and pose keypoints into a compact latent
tensor of shape $`128\times 23\times 11`$. Our design is inspired by
recent work in high-compression autoencoders for diffusion
models \[<a href="#bib.bib5" class="ltx_ref"
title="Deep compression autoencoder for efficient high-resolution diffusion models">7</a>\].
To optimize for real-time performance, the 340M-parameter decoder is
subsequently distilled to a 44M-parameter version by reducing its
upsampling block count, which maintains high reconstruction quality at a
fraction of the computational cost.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS3.SSS1.p2" class="ltx_para">

Player 1’s action history is projected into a dense embedding, encoded
as a multi-hot vector over 8 buttons. This action embedding is summed
with a sinusoidal time embedding for the current diffusion step,
$`t_{\text{emb}}`$, to form the final conditioning vector for the DiT
backbone.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S3.SS3.SSS2" class="section ltx_subsubsection">

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection">3.3.2 </span>Diffusion Transformer Backbone

<div id="S3.SS3.SSS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

The core of our generative model is a 1.2B-parameter Diffusion
Transformer (DiT) \[<a href="#bib.bib4" class="ltx_ref"
title="Scalable diffusion models with transformers">19</a>\], which
learns to denoise and predict future latent frames. The architecture
consists of 16 transformer blocks with a model dimension
$`d_{\text{model}}=2048`$ and 16 attention heads. The conditioning
vector is injected into each block via an Adaptive Layer Normalization
Zero (AdaLNZero) layer, and tokenization is performed using linear
projection layers for spatio-temporal rasterization, bypassing
conventional patch-based embeddings.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS3.SSS2.p2" class="ltx_para">

Each DiT block executes the following sequence:

|  |  |  |  |
|----|----|----|----|
|  | <span id="S3.Ex5.2.1.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_markedasmath">AdaLN</span> | $`\displaystyle\rightarrow\text{Attention}\rightarrow\text{Gated Residual}\rightarrow`$ |  |
|  |  | $`\displaystyle\quad\text{AdaLN}\rightarrow\text{MLP}\rightarrow\text{Gated Residual}`$ |  |

</div>

<div id="S3.SS3.SSS2.p3" class="ltx_para">

To maintain computational tractability over long 128-frame sequences, we
employ a hybrid attention strategy. Most layers use a frame-causal
attention mask with a local sliding window of 16 frames, while every
fourth layer applies global attention across the entire 128-frame
context. This structure balances long-range dependency modeling with
computational efficiency. We apply Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE)
\[<a href="#bib.bib30" class="ltx_ref"
title="RoFormer: enhanced transformer with rotary position embedding">22</a>\]
across both spatial and temporal axes and utilize FlexAttention for an
efficient block-sparse masking implementation.

</div>

</div>

</div>

<div id="S3.SS4" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">3.4 </span>Accelerated Inference for Real-Time Generation

<div id="S3.SS4.p1" class="ltx_para">

Enabling real-time interaction is critical for gaming applications, but
the iterative sampling process of diffusion models is computationally
intensive. To overcome this, we significantly accelerate inference using
two key optimizations.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS4.p2" class="ltx_para">

First, we distill the fully trained model into a few-step sampler using
Distribution Matching Distillation
(DMD) \[<a href="#bib.bib20" class="ltx_ref"
title="Improved distribution matching distillation for fast image synthesis">27</a>\].
We adopt the CausVid DMD framework \[<a href="#bib.bib2" class="ltx_ref"
title="From slow bidirectional to fast autoregressive video diffusion models">28</a>\]
to produce a 4-step distilled model that preserves high generative
fidelity while drastically reducing inference time.

</div>

<div id="S3.SS4.p3" class="ltx_para">

Second, we further enhance speed by implementing static key-value
caching, which reuses previously computed attention states across
generation steps. These optimizations are applied to both the RGB and
visual–pose world models.

</div>

</div>

</div>

<div id="S4" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">4 </span>Experiments

<div id="S4.p1" class="ltx_para">

To validate our claim that a conditional world model can learn reactive
agent behavior from partial observations, we conduct a series of
experiments on the Tekken 3 dataset. We first detail our multi-stage
training pipeline and model architectures. We then introduce our
evaluation benchmarks and present results comparing our primary models
and their distilled variants.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS1" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">4.1 </span>Implementation Details

<div id="S4.SS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

Our training process is divided into three main stages: autoencoder
training, world model training, and distillation for real-time
inference. All models were trained on a cluster of $`8\times`$ NVIDIA
H200 GPUs.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS1.p2" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS1.p2.2.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Stage 1:
Autoencoder Training.</span> We first train a 340M parameter Deep
Compression AutoEncoder (DCAE) to learn a compact latent representation
of the game environment. The autoencoder is trained for 68,000 steps
(approx. 75 hours) on our 1.2 million frame Tekken dataset. It
compresses raw frames ($`3\times 448\times 736`$) into a latent space of
$`23\times 11`$ with 128 channels. The training objective is a
combination of L2 reconstruction loss, perceptual similarity loss, and a
KL divergence term to regularize the latent space. For our
pose-augmented model, we use an identical architecture and training
setup.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS1.p3" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS1.p3.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Stage 2: World
Model Training.</span> We train a 1.2B parameter autoregressive
Diffusion Transformer (DiT) to function as the world model. The DiT
architecture consists of 16 layers, 16 attention heads, and a model
dimension of $`d_{model}=2048`$. It employs a combination of local (16
frames) and global (128 frames) attention windows to capture both
short-term and long-term temporal dependencies. The model is trained on
video clips with a sequence length of 128 frames to predict the next
latent frame conditioned on Player 1’s actions. We train two distinct
world models: one using latents from the RGB-only VAE and another using
latents from the pose-augmented VAE.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS1.p4" class="ltx_para">

<span id="S4.SS1.p4.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Stage 3:
Distillation for Real-Time Inference.</span> To achieve interactive
frame rates, we employ two separate distillation techniques:

- <span id="S4.I1.i1"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S4.I1.i1.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S4.I1.i1.p1.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Decoder
  Distillation:</span> We first create a lightweight VAE decoder for
  real-time rendering. Using student-teacher distillation, we reduce the
  number of upsampling blocks per stage in the decoder from four to one.
  This process reduces the decoder’s parameter count from 340M to a
  nimble 44M.

  </div>
- <span id="S4.I1.i2"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S4.I1.i2.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S4.I1.i2.p1.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Step
  Distillation:</span> We use CausVid, a Distribution Matching
  Distillation (DMD) method, to drastically reduce the number of
  required inference steps for the world model. We distill the
  fully-trained DiT into a 4-step variant. This distillation process
  converges in 2,500 steps, utilizing a combination of a DMD loss and a
  critic loss. We apply this technique to both the RGB-only and the
  pose-augmented world models.

  </div>

</div>

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">4.2 </span>Evaluation Metrics and Benchmarks

<div id="S4.SS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

Evaluating emergent agent behavior presents a fundamental challenge: How
do we measure intelligence that was never explicitly supervised?
Traditional video metrics assess visual fidelity, while RL metrics
assume access to ground-truth actions or rewards. Since COMBAT learns
behavioral patterns implicitly through world modeling, we need novel
evaluation approaches capable of detecting tactical competence from
generated gameplay alone.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS1" class="section ltx_subsubsection">

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection">4.2.1 </span>Standard Perceptual Metrics

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

To assess the perceptual quality of our generated trajectories, we
employ a suite of standard metrics. Our evaluation protocol involves
conditioning the models on real Player 1 action sequences extracted from
a test set of 300 ground-truth videos (roughly 1-2 seconds in length)
consisting of mixed difficulty gameplay. The generated video is then
compared directly against its corresponding ground-truth counterpart
from which the actions were sourced. This setup provides a stringent
test of the model’s ability to render deterministic outcomes based on
specific actions.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS1.p2" class="ltx_para">

We report the Fréchet Video Distance
(FVD)\[<a href="#bib.bib32" class="ltx_ref"
title="FVD: a new metric for video generation">23</a>\] to measure
temporal coherence, the Fréchet Inception Distance
(FID)\[<a href="#bib.bib31" class="ltx_ref"
title="GANs trained by a two time-scale update rule converge to a nash equilibrium">13</a>\]
for per-frame visual fidelity, and LPIPS to quantify perceptual
similarity. Given the high-fidelity nature of the Tekken 3 environment,
achieving strong performance on these metrics against the ground truth
is a robust indicator of the model’s precision and world-modeling
capabilities.

</div>

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection">4.2.2 </span>Behavioral Consistency Metrics

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

To verify that our model learns the game’s intrinsic rules and pacing,
we propose two metrics based on in-game health data:

</div>

<div id="S4.SS2.SSS2.p2" class="ltx_para">

- <span id="S4.I2.i1"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S4.I2.i1.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S4.I2.i1.p1.6.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Damage
  Distribution Analysis:</span> This metric assesses whether the
  consequence of individual actions is realistic. Let $`H^{(t)}_{i}`$
  denote the health of player $`i\in\{1,2\}`$ at frame $`t`$, and define
  per-frame damage as
  $`\Delta H^{(t)}_{i}=\max(0,H^{(t-1)}_{i}-H^{(t)}_{i})`$. We normalize
  by the maximum health $`H^{\text{max}}_{i}`$ to obtain
  $`\delta^{(t)}_{i}=\Delta H^{(t)}_{i}/H^{\text{max}}_{i}`$.

  </div>

  <div id="S4.I2.i1.p2" class="ltx_para">

  The complete distribution of damage values from all generated
  sequences, $`\{\delta^{(t)}_{i,\text{gen}}\}`$, is then compared to
  the distribution from all ground-truth sequences,
  $`\{\delta^{(t)}_{i,\text{real}}\}`$, using the Wasserstein distance.
  A lower distance signifies that the model has learned a more accurate
  mapping from actions to their in-game consequences.

  </div>
- <span id="S4.I2.i2"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S4.I2.i2.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S4.I2.i2.p1.4.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Health
  Trajectory Analysis:</span> This metric evaluates the overall temporal
  flow of the match. Define the normalized time $`s=t/T`$, where $`T`$
  is the total round duration, and let
  $`\bar{H}^{(s)}=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i}H^{(t)}_{i}/H^{\text{max}}_{i}`$ be
  the average normalized health at time $`s`$ for a single round.

  </div>

  <div id="S4.I2.i2.p2" class="ltx_para">

  To establish a baseline for typical match progression, we compute the
  <span id="S4.I2.i2.p2.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">mean health
  trajectory</span> by averaging $`\bar{H}^{(s)}`$ across all rounds in
  our ground-truth test set. We do the same for our generated rounds.
  The similarity between these two mean trajectories is then measured
  using the Mean Squared Error (MSE). A lower MSE indicates that the
  generated gameplay, on average, exhibits a more realistic match pace.

  </div>

</div>

</div>

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3" class="section ltx_subsection">

### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsection">4.3 </span>Human Evaluation of Emergent Behavior

<div id="S4.SS3.p1" class="ltx_para">

To assess the emergent behavior of Player 2, we conduct human evaluation
based on observable action patterns in gameplay. Since Player 2 is
trained without explicit supervision, emergent behavior is defined as
actions that react naturally to Player 1’s inputs, demonstrating
plausible combat strategies such as timely punches, kicks, and defensive
maneuvers.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.p2" class="ltx_para">

We introduce two human-interpretable metrics: <span id="S4.SS3.p2.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Total Action Adherence (TAA)</span> and
<span id="S4.SS3.p2.1.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Action Ratio
Consistency (ARC)</span>. These metrics are based on human annotations
of offensive actions observed in both ground-truth and generated
gameplay sequences.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS1" class="section ltx_subsubsection">

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection">4.3.1 </span>Total Action Adherence (TAA)

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS1.p1" class="ltx_para">

TAA measures whether the agent produces a comparable overall volume of
offensive actions relative to human gameplay:

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS1.p2" class="ltx_para">

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\text{TAA}=\frac{G_{\text{kicks}}+G_{\text{punch}}}{O_{\text{kicks}}+O_{\text{punch}}}
``` |  |

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS1.p3" class="ltx_para">

where $`G_{\cdot}`$ denotes actions performed by the generated agent,
and $`O_{\cdot}`$ the actions performed in original gameplay.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS1.p4" class="ltx_para">

A score of 1.0 indicates perfect adherence in activity level. Scores
$`>1.0`$ suggest hyperactive behavior, while scores $`<1.0`$ indicate
passive behavior.

</div>

#### <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_subsubsection">4.3.2 </span>Action Ratio Consistency (ARC)

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS2.p1" class="ltx_para">

ARC evaluates whether the stylistic balance between punches and kicks
aligns with the human player:

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS2.p2" class="ltx_para">

|  |  |  |
|----|----|----|
|  | 
``` math
\text{ARC}=\frac{\tfrac{G_{\text{punch}}}{G_{\text{kicks}}}}{\tfrac{O_{\text{punch}}}{O_{\text{kicks}}}}
``` |  |

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS2.p3" class="ltx_para">

A score of 1.0 indicates identical punch-to-kick ratio as original
gameplay. Scores above 1.0 reflect stronger preference for punches,
while scores below 1.0 suggest heavier reliance on kicks.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS3.p2" class="ltx_para">

Our evaluation shows that COMBAT successfully learns emergent Player 2
behavior through distinct phases. Initially, the model is hyperactive,
generating nearly four times the offensive actions of human players (TAA
= 3.87), though its punch-to-kick ratio is well-aligned (ARC = 1.04). As
training progresses, the model reduces hyperactivity in further steps.
Beyond step 2000, performance declines, with later checkpoints showing
reduced adherence to original gameplay.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS3.p3" class="ltx_para">

By the final training stages, the model converges toward stable,
human-like combat patterns. It learns to regulate activity frequency
(TAA 1.8) while achieving balanced fighting style (ARC 1.5). However,
overall consistency degrades noticeably.

</div>

<div id="S4.SS3.SSS3.p4" class="ltx_para">

The pose-augmented COMBAT model significantly outperforms the RGB-only
variant across visual quality metrics, confirming that explicit pose
information improves generation quality.

- <span id="S4.I3.i1"><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_item">•</span></span>
  <div id="S4.I3.i1.p1" class="ltx_para">

  <span id="S4.I3.i1.p1.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Impact of
  Distillation:</span> Our 4-step distilled models, created using
  CausVid DMD, retain substantial visual quality while achieving 12.5×
  speedup. The pose-augmented 4-step model still outperforms the full
  RGB-only model, demonstrating efficient distillation with minimal
  quality trade-off.

  </div>

  <div id="S4.I3.i1.p2" class="ltx_para">

  Qualitatively, we observe intelligent behaviors including combo
  execution, spatial awareness, and adaptation to Player 1’s patterns.
  These tactical responses emerge naturally from our training process
  without explicit behavioral supervision.

  </div>

<div id="S5" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">5 </span>Conclusion

<div id="S5.p1" class="ltx_para">

In this work, we introduce COMBAT, a conditional world model that learns
complex, emergent agent behavior from partially observed gameplay. Our
key finding is that by conditioning the model solely on Player 1’s
actions it successfully learns a reactive, tactically coherent policy
for Player 2 without any direct supervision. The model correctly
associates the control inputs with the intended agent and generates
plausible counter-attacks. This demonstrates that intricate behaviors
can arise implicitly from the objective of temporal consistency.

</div>

<div id="S5.p2" class="ltx_para">

We provide an extensive analysis of emergent behavior in world models to
enable further analysis and research. We also release our large-scale
<span id="S5.p2.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">Tekken 3
dataset</span> complete with synchronized pose and segmentation
annotations, and <span id="S5.p2.1.2"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">open-source our pipelines</span> for data
collection and model training.

</div>

<div id="S5.p3" class="ltx_para">

Our approach is practical for interactive entertainment applications.
Through distillation, the COMBAT world model achieves
<span id="S5.p3.1.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">real-time
performance, operating at 85 FPS on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU</span>.
This work represents a contribution as to how generative world models
can learn implicit agent policies, and we hope it inspires further
research into multi-agent behavioral modeling in complex, interactive
environments.

</div>

</div>

<div id="S6" class="section ltx_section">

## <span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_section">6 </span>Future Work

<div id="S6.p1" class="ltx_para">

We identify two primary directions for future research. First, while DMD
step distillation accelerates inference, it degrades agent
responsiveness and attack frequency. Future work should develop
distillation techniques that preserve behavioral fidelity by
incorporating metrics like Action Ratio Consistency (ARC) into the
optimization objective.

</div>

<div id="S6.p2" class="ltx_para">

Second, integrating reinforcement learning (RL) finetuning could guide
the world model toward goal-oriented behaviors like maximizing win-rate.
This involves training a policy <span id="S6.p2.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">within</span> the generative model’s
latent space, establishing a new paradigm for intelligent agents in
simulated environments.

</div>
